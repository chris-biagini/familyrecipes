A crusty, airy artisan loaf with a crackling crust and an open crumb. No kneading required — just time and patience.

Yield: 1 loaf  Adjust: 1x 2x 3x
Prep Time: 20 minutes
Fermentation Time: 12-18 hours
Cook Time: 45 minutes
Total Time: 13-19 hours

MIX THE DOUGH:
- Bread Flour, 500g (4 cups)
- Water (warm), 350g (1½ cups), around 100°F
- Fine Sea Salt, 9g (1½ teaspoons)
- Instant Yeast, 2g (½ teaspoon)

BULK FERMENT:
No additional ingredients for this step.

SHAPE THE DOUGH:
- Rice Flour, 30g (¼ cup), for dusting the banneton

PROOF AND BAKE:
No additional ingredients for this step.

OPTIONAL TOPPINGS:
- Sesame Seeds, 10g (1 tablespoon)
- Flaky Sea Salt, 2g (½ teaspoon)
- Rolled Oats, 15g (2 tablespoons)

Instructions:

1. In a large mixing bowl, combine the bread flour, salt, and instant yeast. Whisk to distribute evenly. Add the warm water and stir with a wooden spoon or your hand until a shaggy, sticky dough forms. There should be no dry flour remaining, but do not overwork the dough.

2. Cover the bowl tightly with plastic wrap or a damp kitchen towel. Let the dough rest at room temperature for 12 to 18 hours. The dough is ready when the surface is dotted with bubbles and it has more than doubled in size. In a cool kitchen this may take closer to 18 hours; in a warm kitchen, 12 hours may be enough.

3. Generously dust a banneton or a medium bowl lined with a clean kitchen towel with rice flour. Flour your work surface and turn the dough out onto it. The dough will be very sticky — that is normal.

4. With floured hands, fold the dough over itself in thirds like a letter, first from top to bottom, then from left to right. Flip the dough seam-side down and use your hands to gently tuck the edges under, forming a round boule. If you'd like toppings, lightly mist the top of the boule with water and press the sesame seeds, flaky salt, or rolled oats onto the surface.

5. Place the dough seam-side up in the prepared banneton. Cover loosely with the towel and let proof for 1 to 2 hours until the dough has risen noticeably and holds a slight indentation when poked with a floured finger.

6. About 30 minutes before the dough is ready, place a Dutch oven with its lid on inside your oven and preheat to 475°F. Let the Dutch oven heat for a full 30 minutes — this is critical for oven spring.

7. Carefully remove the hot Dutch oven. Cut a piece of parchment paper and lay it over the top of the banneton. Invert the banneton so the dough drops gently onto the parchment. Remove the banneton.

8. Using a sharp razor blade or lame, score the top of the loaf with a single confident slash about ¼ inch deep. This controls how the bread expands in the oven.

9. Carefully lower the parchment and dough into the hot Dutch oven. Cover with the lid and bake at 475°F for 30 minutes.

10. Remove the lid and reduce the oven temperature to 425°F. Bake uncovered for an additional 12-15 minutes until the crust is deeply golden brown. The loaf should sound hollow when tapped on the bottom.

11. Transfer the bread to a wire rack and let it cool completely before slicing — at least 1 hour. Cutting into hot bread releases steam and makes the crumb gummy.

Recipe Notes:
- Flour substitute: If you don't have bread flour, use all-purpose flour. The crumb will be slightly less open but still delicious.
- Hydration: If your dough feels too wet during shaping, lightly oil your hands instead of adding more flour, which can tighten the crumb.
- Dutch oven alternative: If you don't have a Dutch oven, bake on a preheated baking stone and place a roasting pan of boiling water on the rack below to create steam.
- Storage: This bread keeps well at room temperature wrapped in a clean towel for 2-3 days. Reheat slices in a toaster or a 350°F oven for 5 minutes to refresh the crust.
