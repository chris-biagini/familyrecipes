# Beef Wellington

Serves: 6
Category: Mains
Tags: holiday

## Sear and rub beef.

- Beef tenderloin (center-cut), about 2 lbs: Trimmed of silverskin and fat.
- Salt (kosher)
- Black pepper
- Vegetable oil, 2 tbsp
- Horseradish (prepared), 2 tbsp
- Mustard (Dijon), 1 tbsp

Tie tenderloin at 1-inch intervals with butcher's twine; season liberally with salt and pepper.

Heat oil in a cast iron or stainless skillet over high until smoking. Sear tenderloin, rotating, until well-browned on all sides, about 10 minutes total. Transfer to a plate.

Mix horseradish, mustard, and 1 tsp black pepper. When cool, cut off twine and rub mixture over all surfaces. Refrigerate uncovered.

## Make duxelles; sear foie gras.

- Mushrooms, 1 lb: Cleaned, trimmed, and roughly chopped.
- Butter (unsalted), 4 tbsp
- Shallots, 2 medium (about 1/2 cup): Finely diced.
- Thyme, 2 tsp: Fresh leaves, finely minced.
- Cognac, 1/2 cup
- Heavy cream, 1/2 cup
- Soy sauce, 2 tsp
- Foie gras (fresh), 4 oz: About 2 1/2-inch slabs.
- Vegetable oil, 1/2 tsp

Pulse mushrooms in two batches in a food processor until very finely chopped, about 10 short pulses per batch.

Melt butter in a 12-inch skillet over medium-high. Add mushrooms; cook, stirring occasionally, until liquid evaporates and mushrooms begin to brown and stick, about 12 minutes. Add shallots and thyme; cook until softened, about 2 minutes. Add cognac, scraping up browned bits, and cook until nearly dry, about 4 minutes. Add cream and soy sauce; cook until mixture is thick and collects in one mass when skillet is shaken. Season with salt and pepper. Transfer to a bowl and refrigerate.

Season foie gras with salt and pepper. Heat oil in a small cast iron or stainless skillet over high until smoking. Sear foie gras without moving until well-browned, about 30 seconds per side. Transfer to a paper towel-lined plate. Pour rendered fat into mushroom mixture; stir to combine and refrigerate. Halve each piece of foie gras horizontally and refrigerate separately. Chill all components at least 30 minutes.

## Assemble the roll.

- Phyllo dough, 1 sheet
- Prosciutto, 1/4 lb: Sliced paper thin.

Lay a double layer of plastic wrap (about 2 feet × 1 foot) on a cutting board. Lay phyllo on top. Shingle prosciutto in a thin, even, overlapping layer, leaving a 2-inch border top and bottom. Spread duxelles evenly over prosciutto.

Place tenderloin along the bottom edge. Arrange sliced foie gras evenly over tenderloin. Using the plastic wrap to tighten, roll tenderloin in the mushroom/prosciutto/phyllo. Rewrap tightly in fresh plastic wrap, twisting the ends to compress into a tight roll. Refrigerate at least 30 minutes.

## Wrap in pastry; chill.

- Flour
- Puff pastry, 14 oz: Thawed.
- Egg, 1: Beaten.

Lightly flour a board and roll puff pastry into a rectangle at least 4 inches wider than the beef roll on its shorter side.

Unwrap beef roll and place foie gras-side up along the bottom edge of the pastry. Brush 6 inches of pastry above the roll with egg wash. Roll beef tightly in pastry until fully wrapped, ending foie gras-side up with the seam at the bottom. Trim excess. Fold in the side flaps to seal the ends; trim the bottom flaps. Flip the roll, fold up end flaps to seal completely, then flip right-side up. Refrigerate at least 30 minutes.

## Bake and serve.

- Coarse sea salt
- Chives, 1 bunch: Finely minced.

Preheat oven to 425°F, rack to center. Transfer Wellington to a foil-lined rimmed baking sheet. Brush all over with remaining egg wash, score a decorative pattern with a paring knife, and sprinkle liberally with coarse sea salt.

Bake until pastry is golden brown and center registers 110°F for rare or 120°F for medium-rare, 35-45 minutes. Rest 10 minutes.

Loosen from foil with a thin spatula and transfer to a carving board. Slice off ends. Carve, sprinkle cut surfaces with chives and more coarse salt, and serve.

---

Center-cut beef tenderloin is also known as Châteaubriand. Mushrooms can be button, cremini, shiitake, portobello, or a mix. Substitute spicy brown or hot English mustard for the Dijon. Bourbon or any barrel-aged spirit can substitute for Cognac. For best results, use an all-butter puff pastry such as Dufour. Foie gras pâté can substitute for fresh foie gras — skip the searing step and spread the pâté directly over the tenderloin during assembly. Special equipment: butcher's twine, instant-read thermometer.
